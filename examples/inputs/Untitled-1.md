he Agricultural Threshold: Why Intelligence Becomes Civilization-Scale Only Through Recursive Environmental Loops
Abstract
Intelligence in the evolutionary sense begins with internal models that enable action across time, not with language, tools, or raw prediction. Many species possess such capacities. What is rare is the alignment of conditions that turn local adaptation into historical transformation. This paper argues that the decisive mechanism is recursive environmental control: the repeated projection of internal models onto the world, intervention according to those models, observation of delayed outcomes, and updating of the model. Agriculture provides the clearest historical example. The same logic applies to artificial intelligence: current systems are powerful predictive components, but genuine higher-order intelligence requires embedding those components in persistent, compounding loops of memory, action, feedback, and externalization. A stricter, falsifiable threshold for AGI is proposed.
Introduction
Intelligence is frequently framed as originating in language, tool use, or predictive power alone. These elements are important but insufficient. In the broader evolutionary sense, intelligence emerges when a living system ceases mere present-moment reaction and begins employing internal models to guide action across extended time horizons. It remembers, predicts, adjusts, and selects behaviors for delayed rather than immediate outcomes. Many non-human species exhibit forms of this capacity; intelligence is therefore not uniquely human. What is rare is the specific alignment of conditions under which intelligence shifts from local niche adaptation to a world-shaping, historically cumulative force.
The key transition is recursive environmental control. A system becomes transformative when it can (1) project a model of future states, (2) act to bring those states about, (3) observe how reality deviates from the model, and (4) revise the model accordingly, creating compounding feedback over long timescales. Cognition thereby ceases to be merely reactive and becomes a self-reinforcing loop that restructures the environment.
Agriculture as the Cognitive Regime Shift
Agriculture did not invent intelligence; the necessary machinery (planning, social learning, delayed-return behavior) predated it. However, farming instantiated the first large-scale, unavoidable, cumulative loop of recursive environmental control. Unlike short-cycle hunting and gathering (detect → pursue → capture → consume), agriculture demands long-horizon projection: planting now for outcomes months later. It requires tracking invisible variables, soil condition, water flow, seasonality, pests, labor allocation, storage, and repeated intervention in an uncertain process.
The mind is no longer merely exploiting existing opportunities; it actively designs future conditions so that reality conforms to an imagined plan. This externalizes and materializes cognition, turning internal models into infrastructure. The frameworks of cultural niche construction and cumulative cultural evolution provide strong empirical support for this view (O’Brien & Laland 2012; Altman et al. 2019; Kendal et al. 2011; Fogarty et al. 2017).
Conditions for Civilization-Scale Intelligence
Many intelligent species never build civilizations because intelligence alone is insufficient. Civilization emerges only when three conditions align:

Loops that systematically reward long-horizon model building.
Cultural transmission mechanisms that preserve and incrementally refine successful strategies across generations.
Environmental manipulation whose effects compound over time (niche construction).

Absent these, intelligence remains biologically bounded. Present, it escapes the individual organism and becomes embedded in tools, practices, institutions, and altered environments, shifting from biological to historical.
Implications for Artificial Intelligence
Current AI systems are routinely described as intelligent, yet this remains imprecise. Most are best understood as cognitive primitives or intelligence components: powerful statistical predictors that compress patterns and generate plausible outputs. Prediction is necessary but not equivalent to the recursive, world-shaping process that drove human civilization.
Agentic systems, those with planning, tool use, and memory modules, already exist in 2025–2026. Defining AGI merely as “predictors embedded in short loops of memory, planning, and tools” would therefore describe the present state of the art. A stricter, falsifiable threshold is required:
A system crosses into higher-order intelligence only when it can

maintain and update relevant state across long time spans and multiple sessions,
convert experience into reusable competence (rather than brittle retrieval),
pursue multi-step goals in open environments with robustness to interruptions and environmental change,
transfer learned procedures across related tasks with minimal manual redesign, and
do so at a reliability level that exceeds current scaffolding.

This threshold mirrors the historical leap in human intelligence: internal models became dominant only when repeatedly externalized through tools, culture, memory systems, and environmental restructuring. Artificial systems will approach comparable status only when predictive components are embedded in persistent, compounding loops that allow accumulation, revision, and genuine reshaping of future conditions.
Conclusion
Today’s AI remains in the early tool phase, astonishingly capable in narrow domains, yet still pre-civilizational in the recursive sense. The next qualitative jump will not come from scale or prediction alone. It will come from systems that can inhabit long feedback loops and improve because of them. In short, the agricultural threshold for machines.
